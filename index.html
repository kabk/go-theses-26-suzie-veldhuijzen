Abstract

This research started when the author noticed that the old stores in her neighborhood were closing and changing into new, hip, artisanal and craft based shops. The author grew up in the Zeeheldenkwartier in The Hague, this neighborhood became very popular among hipsters in the last ten years, and is a clear example of a gentrified neighborhood. The author questions how gentrification is reshaping the current craft culture. And wonders how craft has shifted from an inclusive practice built through experience and knowledge to a performative commercialised version for the elite? This group of elite is described by Jonas Kooymans as the “Havermelkelite”. The Havermelkelite reflects the growing connection between the consumption of artisanal products and identity creation. Are the hipsters using craft as a decor for their lives and identity, and does it matter to them if this is authentic or can it be artificial?

By doing field research inspired by van Jeroen van Bergeijk and Sophie Calle the author works and observes different craft based locations. By visiting the micro bakery Brew and Bake and working at Slagerij Dungelmann and Bakery Meneer Leffers. The author gives an insight on how craft is practiced, performed and presented in different contexts. And questions where the line lies between the "authentic" and “artificial” craft. The field research is supported by theoretical sources like Richard Sennett, Jonas Kooymans, André Hielkema and Tracy Metz. This research document is a mixture of ethnographic observations, autobiographical reflections, and academic discourses. And will work as the foundations for the authors final graduation project.

These are lyrics from the song “De Pijp” by Sophie Straat. The song is part of the album ‘t is niet mijn schuld (It’s not my fault) where Sophie protests against gentrification in the Amsterdam neighborhood de Pijp. She grew up in de Pijp and slowly saw her neighborhood change. At the same time she is aware that she was part of the first generation of gentrification moving to the Pijp. Her mom was one of the first to own a cargo bike in the neighborhood.

I myself moved to the Zeeheldenkwartier in the Hague around twelve years ago. A very up and coming neighborhood. My family and I also took part in gentrifying the neighborhood. Same as Sophie, I saw how this changed my neighborhood. Where there used to be a latex sex shop there is now a Wakuli coffee chain. Where there used to be a gravestone shop there is now a furniture design shop with a window full of midcentury lamps. Many more have changed into artisanal or craft shops. The new wave of hipsters that are moving in and gentrifying the neighborhood, love craft and artisanal things. Artisanal is a product made in a traditional and non-mechanized way and craft is an activity that involves skill and making things by hand.

My way of researching these changes is inspired by the Dutch undercover journalist Jeroen van Bergeijk and French artist Sophie Calle. Jeroen van Bergeijk worked undercover at different jobs like the distribution center of bol. or the call center of Wehkamp, to show people what was happening at the bottom of the Dutch labour market. You can read this in his book Undercover aan het werk or in his columns for the Volkskrant. Sophie Calle doesn't define her projects as art but as a “private game”. For one of her “private games” she worked temporarily as a maid at a Venetian Hotel. And scoured through the personal belongings of the hotel visitors while they were away. She took photos and combined them with text to later publish to the public. She trespasses people's privacy and uses voyeurism and surveillance as her primary artistic tactics. For my research I am not going to trespass peoples privacy but I will work in places where I don’t actually work or visit places where I don’t fit in. To get an exclusive look inside to see how craft businesses are run. And why there is such a longing for them?

Brew and Bake

It’s 13:30 and I’m going to a cafe on the Spuistraat in The Hague, the cafe opened two weeks ago. It is a micro bakery, I don’t know what that means. On the outside window it says the name of the cafe Brew and Bake in bold red lettering. Their logo is an abstract red bow.

I stand in line. In front of me there are people wearing the newest and hippest stuff.

I see 3 mullets,

4 moustaches,

2 Carhartt hats,

3 Freitag bags,

2 yoga mats

and outside there are 3 cargo bikes parked.

Some people are there for a take away coffee, some for some fresh sourdough and others for a nice kardamon pastry and an iced matcha. I can tell by the people standing in line this is a hip place. I can also tell that I might not fit in. I do try to fit into this group of people though. I also own a Freitag bag, do a creative design and only work with Apple. Still I don’t know the unwritten rules of this place and I don’t seem to care enough about sourdough to stand in line.

The people in line are the new hipsters. Dutch journalist Jonas Kooymans, author of the book De havermelkelite gave a name to these new hipsters. In his book he calls these new hipsters “de Havermelkelite” (the Oatmilkelite). Since drinking oat milk is an expression of a conscious and sustainable lifestyle. He describes the Oatmilkelite as twenty/thirty year olds with pufferjackets, Airpods and e-bikes wearing Salomon sneakers or Birkenstocks in the summer. These Oatmilkelite are different to the generation of hipsters from the era before (2001-2011). Recognizable by their skinny jeans, boho dresses, man buns, scarves, lace and their love for retro items. This new generation of hipsters cares more about wine, self help, the climate, pilates, protein and what their image is on social media. They have become more individualistic then the hipsters from before and often have no kids. They also have more money and could be considered as an elite group.

The term hipsters goes back to the 1940’s. Where the word hipster emerged in the African American community as a synonym for “hepcat”. Which refers to jazz enthusiasts. All these generations of hipsters are busy with finding the newest cultural trends. And feel that they have a better understanding of culture than the masses.

Whenever I go to a place like Brew and Bake I feel like I have to adjust. I have to show that I also know the newest latest trends, by the clothes I wear and the coffee I buy.

Meneer Leffers

At 07:00 I arrive at Meneer Leffers, a bakery on the Herengracht in The Hague. Meneer Leffers is a bakery where they make their own fresh bread and pastries in the morning to later sell in their shop. I worked there for 4 hours. My colleague Giulia was already there. Guila is a Spanish woman and works full time as a baker at Meneer Leffers. Since she was there earlier than me she started the baking process already and was taking the first pastries out of the oven. She explains that in the morning the timer is her boss. And she is just working from timer to timer.

The pastries were scones, sweet and savory once. She takes a two litre carafe filled with batter out of the fridge. “Have you ever had a canelle?” she asked me. “I have not” I answer. She explains that canelle is a small cake like pastry invented by nuns. When back in the day they wanted to sift pulp out of wine they would pour it through egg white. That meant they had a lot of the yellow from the eggs left over. The nuns turned this into a batter, and poured it into little tower shaped tins, to cook it at a very high temperature. Originally the nuns would butter the tins with bee wax, but at Meneer Leffers they don’t do that. They use spray butter.

While the cannelle were in the oven I put a sugar and lemon mixture on the sweet scones. The mixture was like thick honey and melted over the top of the scone.

Emma walks in. She is the other baker working together with Guilia. Giulia asks her about the missing chocolate cookies. Emma explains that yesterday they did not have enough time to prepare the cookies. They politely argued about the chocolate cookies for fifteen minutes. Not blaming each other but most definitely not blaming themselves.

While still bickering Emma starts a new batch of desem bread. She puts the flour mixture that they measured the day before, in a big industrial mixer and adds water from a tube. When the dough is mixed she touches and feels the dough, testing if it's not too stretchy or too dry. When she decides the dough is good she divides it into plastic boxes to start the proofing process. With the proofing everything is also ruled by the timers, fifteen minutes of resting, then folding the dough four times. Repeating this process at least three times until Emma and Guilia decide the dough is good.

I talk with Emma about the future of craft. She is afraid that machines will take over human jobs. And that in the future small shops like Meneer Leffers won’t exist anymore and jobs like hers will disappear. I don’t think that will happen. Because what she is afraid of, that machines would be able to do what she does, but on a much bigger scale and for a lower price, has already happened. Bread and pastries are already mass produced.

I believe people will keep coming to small shops where other people make their food. Where you have to wait in line for a bit and when it’s your turn, you have a chat. Apparently one of the regulars at Meneer Leffers broke up with her boyfriend. And another one is moving out. These personal interactions are what makes customers come back. People pay more to experience going to a little shop where they can see others working, to then buy something from people they trust.

American journalist, Tracy Metz, who lives in the Netherlands wrote the book Pret! Leisure and landschap. In the book she explains that nowadays cultural historical craft practices or objects can’t just be. They have to make money and be consumed. Just cherishing a historical object or act is not enough anymore for them to stay existing. You have to experience them. And with the rise in nostalgia and longing for authenticity there is a threat that this will evoke artificialness. A pretend version of what used to be. The experience around the product is more important than the actual product.

The gentrified craft shops use their customers' nostalgia to sell upmarket products to their specific groups with a specific budget. They use craft as part of their brand identity even though they're not practicing it. With their big windows they highlight the artificial craft that is being done.

Jonas Kooyman says in his book that he feels the people, working and visiting, the gentrified bakeries are putting up a play. A play romanticizing the working class, you call this poverty play. They're pretending to live like the working class which would stereo typically be authentic and pure. And that’s why they go to the artisanal bakery for that authentic bread. Even though the bread is six times more expensive than at the supermarket and the real working class can’t even afford this authentic bread.

Slagerij Dungelmann

Every Saturday at 8:45 I arrive at work. I give Rob a fistbump and say hi to the rest. I put my jacket downstairs and put on my apron. On the front of my apron it says “Slagerij Dungelmann". This is the name of the butchery where I work. Slagerij Dungelmann is well known in the Hague, we are also Purveyors to the Royal Household. Which means that sometimes we deliver products to the royal family.

I start cutting open over 200 sandwiches, filling the salad bar, slicing cold meat, checking dates on meals or putting raw meatballs on plates. Most days I stand at the sandwich counter. Where people come to get their broodje kroket or meatball (what we are known for) but also for a sandwich with roast beef or halfom. After working there for four years my favorite sandwich is a cheese sandwich.

Nowadays I work way more in the front with the raw meat. Here I get close to the actual craft of the butchery. I learned the basics by copying my colleagues and then just doing it. To be a butcher it is nice to have spatial awareness. You need to be able to see what 300 grams of rib-eye is and then cut it off with confidence. I know my hand can grab 450 grams of ground beef. Which is annoying because people always want 500 grams. But there is much more to the craft than just slicing the meat in the correct amount.

I never expected to work in a butchery. Let alone work there over four years and enjoy it as much as I do. I am proud of the butchery, my colleagues are too. Proud to keep the craft alive even though it’s not always easy. Tax and energy prices rising makes it tough to stay positive about the future of the shop. Many old family craft businesses closed because of these reasons. But we keep going and we evolve with the times. We even sell vegan sandwiches now

My boss is the only one in the shop who has a degree to be a butcher. His degree is still from the time when being a butcher was a protected craft, and you couldn't practice it without a degree. From 1996 until 2007 these rules loosened and eventually disappeared. Many crafts like the butcher, baker, hairdresser, beauty specialist or mechanic became unprotected crafts. This was to make entry to the labor market easier for people. That means anyone could open a hairdressing salon now. Adam, a colleague of mine, quit his communications study last year. This year he started the butcher's education. He found out by working at Dungelmann that this was his passion and he wanted to study the craft further.

In the sixteenth century the crafts were also protected, they were protected by the guilds. The guilds were organisations for each of the specific workshops like the butcher, smith, baker or tailor they checked them on their quality and price. The guilds made sure there were no price battles between the bakeries in the city and they would all have equal prices for their customers. The workshops had to pay to be a part of the guild in exchange for social and financial security.

In the craft workshops you had the master, the owner and boss of the workshop. Under him you had the journeyman who was the master's left hand. He had a lot of knowledge about the craft but not enough experience to be the master. And you had the apprentice who was there to learn everything. Learning by copying the others, that was the way of getting skills. Richard Sennet explains in his book the Craftsman that with getting skills, information and practice comes together into tactic knowledge. Which is a type of knowledge which is personal and difficult to transfer to others. It’s based on your own experience and intuition. He explains that in higher stages of skill there is a constant interplay between this tactic knowledge and self-conscious awareness. For the workshops knowledge was their capital.

The guild system eventually disappeared after the industrial revolution started. More people moved to the cities to work in the factories. These factories started producing more products, faster and for smaller prices. The mass production of products made the purpose of the products change. And with all these new choices people became more aware of their own being. Not who they are on the inside, but aware of what they portray on the outside. People became able to create their identity with the products they bought and started to buy to be. Often mimicking others.

Ari Versluis and Ellie Uyttenbroek are two artists who visualize this, in their project Exactitudes. They do this by photographing and then categorizing people from all different social types. Selecting them on their appearance. Their focus is on investigating the way cultures, sub-cultures and social types produce and spread meaning in our common spaces. What symbols do they give off and how do they show those? Putting the people in front of the same white background, to then order them in a grid system. Now you can see clearly how these people show their identity not by who they are on the inside but by what they show on the outside.

The hipsters put themselves in a grid system too. By the people outside in line at Brew and Bake with their mullets, moustaches, Carhartt hats, Freitag bags, yoga mats and cargo bikes I could tell right away which social group I was interacting with. The shop works like the grid system and gives these people a space to come together.

But I didn’t seem to care enough about sourdough to stand in line. That is what I felt when I did stand in line at Brew and Bake. I wasn't waiting for just bread. I was standing in line for a product that gave me a way to curate my identity. And show others by buying sourdough where I believed I stood in society. But that is not meaningful to me.

Craft has shifted from a practice built through experience and knowledge, that is there for everyone to enjoy, to a commercialised and aesthetic version for the elite. Gentrified spaces turned craft into an artificial experience tied to identity and exclusivity. By mimicking what used to be. I see the value of craft in others like my boss, Guilia and Emma who are still carrying on the origin of craft with skills, knowledge, human connection and inclusivity. That is why I will keep visiting and working in the workshops of what I believe true craftsmen are.
