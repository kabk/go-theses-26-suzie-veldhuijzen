A Piet Mondrian amount of abstract abstract…

A Piet Mondrian amount of abstract abstract… Gentrification is changing neighborhoods. In the Zeeheldenkwartier where I grew up, many stores have changed. Where there used to be a latex sex shop there is now a hipster coffee shop. Where there used to be a gravestone shop there is now a designer furniture shop. Many more have changed. These new hip places play into a nostalgia back to a time with craftsmanship everywhere around you. Quality products made by people who had all the knowledge. In the 16th century knowledge was your capital Richard Sennett says in his book The Craftsman. At my job in the butchery this is still the case. My boss teaches me and my other colleagues all the skills he has. But the necessity of craft that it had back then is now disappearing. With the start of the industrial revolution, mass consumption started. And people became able to buy anything they wanted for a small price. This changed the purpose of the products we buy, André Hielkema mentions in his text Het raadsel van de moderne consumptie. People became trained into always wanting more and getting new products to fit in with others. With this abundance of products on the market you see a counter movement. Young well educated people start longing back to craft products. Craft is now in and cool. You see this in the rise of craft related shops in my parents neighborhood. But the shops the hip people go to are all brand new. They don’t go to the local family driven bakery but the fancy hip place with the well done graphic design. How do these artificial craft shops affect the older shops? I am going to work in different types of crafts and see what the people do. Who comes to their stores, how warm it is inside and how do they deal with the ever changing market.

start

At seventeen years old I started working in a butchery. I would make the sandwiches for the customers. With for example, kroket or a meatball but also roast beef or halfom. I’ve worked there for over four years now. Now I also work at the actual butchery station. So people will order their ground beef, veal sausages or tenderloin with me. I give them the amount they ask for with what they call the butchers margin of 20 grams more or less, most often more. And tell them how to prepare it with a little story about how tender the meat is. I learned a lot about meat and the craft of the butchery by working there. Not really because they explained it to me but because I listen, pay attention and learn by doing. Customers trust you, as long as you're behind the counter and wearing the uniform with the name: Dungelmann since 1862 on it. I love working there. I love my colleagues, I love our regulars and I love the passion that everyone has for the place. Craft to me is very important. Not only the artistic craft which I learned from my grandma but also the crafts you see in the street. The small places often owned by families where people come to socialise and get products they trust from people they trust. I love the sharing of knowledge in places of crafts. There is always something new to learn from vendor to customer and the other way around. I moved to the Zeeheldenkwartier around 12 years ago. This is a neighborhood in the center of The Hague. The neighborhood has become very popular the last few years under a new generation of hipsters. And this affected the stores in the street. Where there used to be a latex sex shop there is now a hipster coffee shop. Where there used to be a gravestone shop there is now a designer furniture shop. And many more have changed into artisanal, craft or vintage shops. I go to a cafe to write. It is a micro bakery, I don’t know what that means. On the outside window it says the name of the cafe ‘BREW AND BAKE’ in bold red lettering. Their logo is an abstract red bow. I stand in line. In front of me there are people wearing all the newest hippest stuff. Most of them are waiting and looking at their I-Phone. I see 3 mullets, 4 moustaches, 2 carhartt hats, 6 freitag bags, 2 yoga mats and outside there are 3 cargo bikes parked. Some people are here for a take away coffee, some for some fresh sourdough and others for a nice kardamon pastry and an iced matcha. From the people in the line I can tell that this place is hip. I can also tell that I might not fit in. I do try to fit into this group of people though. I also own a freitag bag, wear baggy jeans and only work with Apple. I do a creative design study and I am friends with internationals. Still I don’t know the unwritten rules of this place and I don’t seem to care enough about sourdough to stand in line. Jonas Kooymans gave a name to this group of people “de Havermelk elite” the Oatmilk elite. He describes them as twenty/thirty year olds with pufferjackets, Airpods, and e-bikes wearing Salomon sneakers or in the summer Birkenstocks. They are a different generation than the hipster from the era before. Who were way more rough around the edges. This generation cares about wine, self help, the climate, pilates, protein and what their image is on social media. They are often well educated and work in the creative field. So when I go to a place like brew and bake I feel like I have to adjust. I was thinking of ordering a matcha since everybody seems to drink those nowadays and I wanted to try it. But I ordered a black coffee, I always order a black coffee. I did order a croissant, it looked very shiny and fluffy on the counter so I thought why not. I sit down inside, on a heavy metal chair. It is the type of chair that you don’t want to sit on when you're wearing shorts. First your ass will freeze from the cold metal and then your legs will start slipping around from the sweat accumulating on the chair. I got a buzzer device for my order, a little machine that will start vibrating and making a sound when my order is done. The place looks very minimalistic and a bit cave like. With grey lime washed walls and wooden or metal decor. In the middle of the store stands a big tree. There are few merchandise stands. Selling coffee and matcha with the brew and bake logo on it. The place opened around two weeks ago. And it looks very sleek. There are around six people working. Young hip people. With colorful branded hats, moustaches or short bangs. I never know at these places if I should order in Dutch or in English. I often just order in English since I’m sure we both speak that language. My buzzer goes off, a high pitched peep peep peep sound and flashing red lights. I have to walk to the counter to get my order. My coffee and croissant are served on a tiny wooden tray which I balance in one hand at my table. When I sit back down I take a bite of my croissant. It’s dry. And the shininess that intrigued me to get it, is a layer of sugar. I don’t like sweet croissants. So that's a bummer. The coffee is good. I look around me. People are sitting here working, studying and chit chatting. There is ambient music playing from a giant jbl speaker with green flashing lights. This giant speaker is the only thing where you can tell that they are just starting up and haven't figured out their sound system yet. The speaker is playing a playlist with artists like Mac Miller and Oliva Dean. But the main sound I keep hearing is the beeping of those buzzers. It is not very busy in the cafe. But the combination of buzzers, people trying to move their heavy chairs over the concrete floor and the milk foam machine slashing is not very relaxing. So I put in my headphones to focus. I put on my playlist, which is the same music as they were playing in the cafe with Mac Miller and Olivia Dean.
